# Evidence-Based Cycling Training for the Flat Century

**Athlete profile:** ~40 yo male, FTP 260 W (~3.4 W/kg), target events ~100 mi / 160 km with ~500 ft / 150 m climbing.

**Method:** Fan-out web research across 6 angles → 29 sources → 131 candidate claims → 25 adversarially verified (3-vote, kill on 2/3 refute). 21 confirmed, 4 refuted. Confidence labels below reflect that verification, not my opinion.

---

## TL;DR — what the evidence actually supports

1. **Build a big aerobic base, keep it ~80/20.** ~80% of your riding genuinely easy, ~20% moderate-to-hard. No periodization model beats another over 8–12 weeks in trained riders — consistency and volume win. **[High confidence]**
2. **Add heavy lower-body strength training. This is the single best-supported "extra."** ~80–85% 1RM, ~4 exercises, 2×/week, 12+ weeks. It improves economy and — most relevant to you — **durability**: ~7–8% more power _at the end_ of a 3+ hour ride. **[High confidence]**
3. **Skip moderate big-gear/low-cadence "strength" intervals.** A 12-week RCT in veteran cyclists (mean age 47) found zero benefit, and claims of low-cadence superiority were refuted. **[High confidence]**
4. **For masters athletes, maintained training volume — not age — is the dominant lever.** Most of the age-related fitness decline tracks dropping volume, not biology. **[Medium confidence]**
5. **Fueling and pacing** (below) rest on coaching/practitioner consensus, not RCTs that survived verification — treat as well-established best practice, not proven-for-you.

## 1. Training structure & intensity distribution

**Verdict: pyramidal is the natural default for cycling; polarized is a viable alternative; the difference is small. The 80/20 split underpins both.** _[High confidence]_

- A 2024 meta-analysis (Sun/Oliveira et al., n≈284–437) found **polarized (POL) superior only for VO₂peak** (SMD = 0.24, p = 0.04), and that edge was bigger in _highly trained_ athletes (SMD = 0.46) and _short_ (<12 wk) blocks. **POL was NOT superior for time-trial performance** (SMD = −0.01, p = 0.92).
- A 2023 systematic review (7 studies) found **no periodization model favored over 8–12 weeks** in trained cyclists.
- Cycling naturally tends toward a **pyramidal** distribution (lots of low intensity, a moderate amount of threshold, a little high-intensity). The **80/20 principle** (~80% low-intensity, 5–10% anaerobic) underpins both pyramidal and polarized.
- A 16-week pyramidal program (~57/31/12) in **recreational men, mean age 41** — the closest match to you — raised power at the 4 mmol lactate threshold with large effect sizes (d = 1.21 absolute, d = 1.54 W/kg). _Caveat: single-arm, uncontrolled, n=14 — shows pyramidal works, not that it's superior._
- A seasonal **pyramidal → polarized sequence** produced the largest gains, but that evidence comes from elite _runners_, not cyclists.

**What was refuted:** A widely-cited claim that polarized "outperforms" threshold and high-volume approaches in well-trained athletes was refuted 0-3. A separate network meta-analysis found recreational athletes actually respond _better_ to pyramidal. So don't believe anyone selling polarized as strictly best.

**Practical takeaway for you:**

- Default to **pyramidal**: a large base of Zone 2, a meaningful block of sweet-spot/threshold (Zone 3–low 4), and a smaller amount of VO₂-max work.
- Sweet-spot vs. polarized is a smaller decision than _total consistent volume_. Pick the one you'll actually adhere to.
- For a flat century specifically, your limiter is **sustained sub-threshold power and fatigue resistance**, not top-end VO₂max — which argues for a pyramidal/sweet-spot lean over heavily polarized.

## 2. Strength training — the highest-value addition

**Verdict: heavy lower-body strength training reliably improves cycling performance, economy, and durability. Best-supported supplementary intervention for your profile.** _[High confidence]_

**The protocol that works (from the studies):**

- **~80–85% 1RM** (~6–8 rep max), **~4 exercises** (squat / leg press dominant), **~3–4 sets**, **~2×/week**, for **~12–14 weeks**.
- Heavy, low-rep, high-force — **not** light-weight high-rep "endurance" lifting.

**What it does:**

- 2025 EJAP meta-analysis (17 studies, 262 cyclists): heavy strength (≥80% 1RM) improved **performance** (ES = 0.46, p = 0.016) and **economy/efficiency** (ES = 0.35, p = 0.012), via improved economy and anaerobic power — **without raising VO₂max**. _Caveat: GRADE certainty LOW._
- Sunde et al. 2010 RCT: 8 weeks of 4×4RM half-squats improved **cycling economy 4.8%** and **time-to-exhaustion at max aerobic power 17.2%**.

**The century-relevant part — durability:** _[High confidence]_

- Rønnestad et al. 2011 RCT (20 well-trained cyclists): 12 weeks heavy strength raised 5-min all-out power **after 185 minutes of cycling** from 371 → 400 W (**~7.8%**), with reduced VO₂, HR, lactate, and RPE in the final hour. The endurance-only group didn't change.
- Vikmoen et al. 2017: 11 weeks heavy strength improved **post-3-hour cycling performance by 7.0%** and lowered VO₂/HR in the final 2 hours.
- This is exactly the quality you need at mile 80–100 of a century.

**Important caveat:** these studies used well-trained (and in one case female-only) athletes with mean age ~31, not 40-year-old recreational males. The benefit transfers **by inference**, not by direct demonstration in your exact demographic. Note also: the research **did not** substantiate specific injury-prevention claims — durability/economy are the proven wins.

**Practical takeaway:**

- Run a **12+ week strength block**, ideally in base/off-season, tapering frequency (not abandoning it) as you approach event-specific volume.
- Squat / leg press / deadlift / hip hinge, heavy, 2×/week. Treat it as training, not an afterthought.

## 3. Low-cadence / big-gear "torque" intervals

**Verdict: NOT supported at moderate intensity. Don't build your plan around them.** _[High confidence]_

- Frontiers 2014 RCT: 22 well-trained **veteran male cyclists (mean age 47)** did 12 weeks of **40 rpm intervals at 73–82% HRmax**, 2×/week → **no improvement** in aerobic capacity, performance, power, _or leg strength_.
- Three separate claims that low-cadence beats high-cadence (peak power, 60-s power, power at 4 mM lactate) were each **refuted 0-3**.

**Important scope note:** this null result is for **moderate-intensity** big-gear work. It does _not_ rule out higher-intensity big-gear efforts (sprint-interval / HIIT done in a big gear) — those simply weren't the tested condition, so they're unproven rather than disproven. If you want force/strength adaptations, the barbell (Section 2) is the evidence-backed route, not grinding 40 rpm on the road.

**Practical takeaway:** Self-selected cadence is fine. Don't spend training time on moderate low-cadence grinding expecting a strength or threshold payoff — lift instead.

## 4. Masters (40+) considerations

**Verdict: maintained training volume — not age itself — is the dominant _modifiable_ driver of fitness.** _[Medium confidence]_

- Valenzuela et al. 2022: changes in **training volume explained 54% of VO₂max-decline variance in male masters athletes** (39% in females). Translation: most of what looks like "aging" is actually training less.
- Caveats: narrative review (regression over ~6 studies); ~46% of variance unexplained; an irreducible aging "slow component" of roughly **−5 to −6.5% per decade** persists even with full training maintenance. Verification vote was 2-1.

**What the research did _not_ verify** (flagged honestly): specific protocols for testosterone decline, slower recovery, or how exactly to modify weekly load for a 40-year-old. The surviving evidence supports one robust principle — **protect your consistency and volume** — more than any age-specific hack.

**Practical takeaways (consensus, lower evidentiary weight):**

- Prioritize **uninterrupted consistency** over any single hard session. The masters athlete's enemy is the missed week, not the missed interval.
- You likely tolerate **slightly less high-intensity frequency** and need **more recovery** between hard days — but the data point is "keep volume up," not "go easy."
- Strength training (Section 2) doubles as a masters anti-aging intervention (muscle mass, force, economy).

## 5. Fueling & pacing for a flat century

**Confidence: LOWER.** No peer-reviewed claim on this survived adversarial verification — the fetched sources were practitioner/blog quality. The following is **well-established sports-nutrition consensus**, not RCT-proven-for-you. Treat as a strong default to test in training, never on race day.

**Fueling (a 5–7 hour effort):**

- **Carbohydrate: ~60–90 g/hour** for efforts over ~2.5 hours. The upper end (90+, even 100–120 g/hr) requires a **2:1 glucose:fructose** mix to clear the gut and **a trained gut** — practice it in long rides first.
- **Start fueling early** (within the first 30–45 min), before you feel you need it. Bonking is a deficit you can't repay mid-ride.
- **Hydration:** ~500–750 mL/hour, more in heat; include **sodium** (~300–700 mg/L) on long/hot days.
- A flat century is a **fueling and pacing test as much as a fitness test** — the steady power demand makes under-fueling the most common failure mode.

**Pacing (flat, ~500 ft):**

- Target a **steady normalized power well below FTP** — roughly **65–75% of FTP** (low Zone 3 / high Zone 2) for a 5–7 hour ride. At 260 W FTP that's ~170–195 W average.
- **Even/steady pacing** beats variable on flat terrain — surges cost disproportionate glycogen. Resist the temptation to chase wheels early.
- On flat courses, **drafting is the single biggest free saving** — sitting in a group can cut your power demand 20–30% at the same speed. Group selection > individual heroics.

_(These are flagged as open questions in the research — worth a dedicated evidence dive if you want RCT-grade numbers.)_

## 6. Raising FTP / W/kg from 3.4

**Confidence: LOWER for specific numbers.** The research only verified general training-_structure_ findings, not concrete FTP-raising prescriptions. Below blends those verified findings with standard coaching practice (flagged).

**From the verified evidence:**

- The pyramidal program in age-41 recreational men (Section 1) **raised threshold power**, including in W/kg terms (d = 1.54) — so structured sweet-spot/threshold work demonstrably moves your exact metric.
- Strength training raises **power without weight gain** (no VO₂max change, improved economy) — favorable for W/kg.

**Two levers on W/kg:**

1. **Power (watts):** structured threshold/sweet-spot blocks + VO₂max work + the strength block above.
2. **Weight (kg):** since W/kg is a ratio, body-composition changes can move it as fast as power gains — but don't compromise the fueling/recovery that drives training quality.

**Practical sweet-spot template (coaching consensus, not RCT-verified):**

- 2× quality sessions/week of **sweet-spot/threshold** intervals (e.g., 2–4 × 10–20 min at 88–95% FTP), progressively overloaded.
- A periodic **VO₂max block** (e.g., 5 × 3–5 min near max) to lift the ceiling FTP sits under.
- Everything else **easy Zone 2** to support the 80/20 structure.
- **Re-test every 6–8 weeks** and adjust zones.

Getting from 3.4 → 4.0 W/kg is realistic over a season for a consistent recreational rider, but it's driven by **volume + structured threshold work + strength + body composition**, all compounding — not any single workout type.

## Honest limitations of this report

- Key strength and intensity studies used **well-trained/elite or female cohorts, mean age ~31** — not a 40-yo recreational male. Benefits transfer **by inference**.
- The one demographically-matched study (age-41 recreational men) was **uncontrolled (n=14)** — shows pyramidal _works_, not that it's _best_.
- The strength meta-analysis carries **LOW GRADE certainty**.
- The polarized-vs-pyramidal literature is **genuinely mixed**; I've represented that rather than picking a side.
- **Fueling/pacing (§5) and specific FTP numbers (§6) are coaching consensus, not verified RCT findings** — the weakest-evidenced sections, clearly the place to dig deeper if you want certainty.

## Bottom line

For _you_ specifically: **(1)** ride a lot, mostly easy, pyramidal structure; **(2)** add a heavy 12-week strength block — it's your biggest durability win for the back half of a century; **(3)** drop moderate big-gear intervals; **(4)** guard your consistency above all as a masters athlete; **(5)** rehearse fueling (~60–90 g carbs/hr) and steady sub-threshold pacing in training; **(6)** sweet-spot/threshold blocks plus body composition move W/kg.
